Meet Gru & Scope Your App: The /v0 Gate

Ciao — this is Liam. Episode 1 of 4. Before you can evaluate an AI system, you have to specify one precisely. Gru is the fastest way to build that muscle — and its first gate will stop a vague idea cold. Good. This week runs in four episodes: scope your app in Gru, build the full SDD, generate and read the Boondoggle Score, then write the reflection and ship. Every episode has a concrete deliverable. This one has one job: your app passes the /v0 gate. Let's go. Gru is a software-design-documentation system built on the Irreducibly Human curriculum. You load it as a Claude Project with the Gru system prompt, or you paste the prompt directly into a new session and run /help to confirm it loaded. When /help returns the list of phase commands — /v0 through /s1 — Gru is active. It is not a chatbot. It is a phase-gate engine: it will not move forward until each gate passes. That distinction matters. A chatbot will accept a vague idea and generate something that looks like output. Gru will refuse and tell you exactly what is missing. The gates are: /v0 names the thing being built. /v1 through /v4 build the problem summary, principles, flows, and need statements. /s1 assembles the full Software Design Document. Each gate requires the previous one to pass. You cannot jump to /s1 from /v0. The order is the curriculum.

What you'll see:
• Before you run /v0, you need an app worth scoping. Good candidates share three properties: a specific domain, a specific user making a specific decision, and an output that is a document, score, flag, or structured artifact — not a conversation. Examples: a finance claim flagger that checks submitted invoices for policy violations, an annotation tool that routes ambiguous medical images to the right reviewer, a clinic scheduler that assigns appointment slots based on patient triage priority, a moderation dashboard that surfaces borderline posts for human review, a test generator that produces domain-specific assessment questions from a syllabus, a calibration tracker that monitors how a team's confidence scores compare to their outcomes. Anti-patterns: 'an AI assistant for X' — too broad, no insertion point, no output you can test. Two or three generic components — too simple, Gru will flag the missing domain knowledge immediately. And thin domain knowledge — if you cannot describe the domain's edge cases, the SDD will expose that. The test before you run /v0: can you name the domain, the user, the decision, and the output in one sentence? If you need two sentences, the scope is still loose.
• Run /v0. The gate asks you to name the thing being built — not the problem it solves, not the user it helps, but the thing itself. The exact naming convention: a noun phrase that describes the artifact or system, followed by its insertion point, followed by its output. 'A claim-flagging tool that sits between the invoice intake form and the payment queue and produces a binary pass or flag with a violation reason' passes. 'An AI that helps finance teams with compliance' does not pass — it names a problem, not a thing. If the gate stops you, that is the assignment working. Do not argue with Gru's pushback. Rewrite the one-sentence naming until it is specific enough that the gate passes. Every rewrite is progress — you are learning to specify, not to negotiate. The students who get frustrated by /v0 pushback are the same students who will miss the score's hardest handoffs in Episode 3. The gate is training your eye.
